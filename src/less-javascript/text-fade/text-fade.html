Smooth Text Fade

The old trick was to layer a white gradient over the bottom of the text. That breaks in dark mode and on textured backgrounds. CSS masks fade the text itself, so it works anywhere.

The quietest revolutions in software rarely arrive with a press release. They show up as a single CSS property, a one-line patch, a tiny primitive that quietly replaces a tower of JavaScript glue. You wake up one morning, open the docs, and realize the workaround you've been shipping for years is now a one-liner native to the platform.

Masks are one of those primitives. Instead of painting a colored rectangle over your content to hide it, you tell the browser which pixels of the element itself should be visible. A gradient becomes a dimmer switch for opacity. Suddenly the same fade works on a white page, a dark page, a photograph, a grid of stripes, a video.

You stop thinking in terms of "what color is the background" and start thinking in terms of "what shape do I want this content to have." It's a small shift in framing that opens up an enormous space of effects you used to need canvas, SVG, or JavaScript for.

How it works

.fade-text-container {
  max-height: 120px;
  overflow: hidden;
  mask-image: linear-gradient(
    to bottom,
    #000 40%,
    transparent 100%
  );
}
  • max-height + overflow: hidden clip the paragraph to a preview.
  • mask-image works like a Photoshop layer mask: black keeps pixels, transparent erases them.
  • The gradient stays solid for the top 40%, then fades to nothing — so the text dissolves instead of being covered.
